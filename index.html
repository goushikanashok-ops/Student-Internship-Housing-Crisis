About this report

What we care about and why

This report examines the economic, geographic, racial and institutional barriers to professional opportunity for low-income students. We don't believe that housing should decide who gets a job.

This report explores financial, geographical, racial, and institutional obstacles to achieving professional pathways for low-income students. We believe that there should be no link between housing and a job. According to a NACE survey, 52.5 percent of graduating college students finished internships during their college years, with pay status splitting the results. 61 percent of the paid interns received job offers upon graduation. Only 38 percent of the unpaid interns had. That 23-point difference demonstrates that the internship system doesn't just reward experience — it rewards the financial ability to work for free.

"The system isn't rewarding talent. It's rewarding the ability to pay."

The problem

Why students can't afford to intern

Once students get into competitive internships, there is another hurdle: geography. The vast majority of high-paying internships are in cities such as Los Angeles, which is home to a staggering imbalance between jobs and housing, as indicated in the map below. Those lacking the financial means to reside close to the job centre are placed on the fringes of the metro, where they have to travel a long distance to their work location, creating a highly expensive and exhausting commute as well. A lower priced apartment doesn't always translate to a lower price for your summer when you're paying for gas and transportation daily.

A way forward

Structural fixes, not individual workarounds

Students, employers, and universities must co-work, along with policymakers, to address the internship housing challenge. Establish summer housing networks at universities to link students to existing summer housing facilities in the student's internship locations. Employers need to offer commute scores, vetted housing lists, and get interns to transit, before signing a lease. Internships should begin at the bare bones level, paid or fully funded — no student should be putting themselves in debt to enhance their resume.

01
Require paid or fully funded internships Universities should not allow students to take internships for credit unless the internship pays minimum wage or provides a stipend covering housing, food, and transportation.
02
Build a university-to-university housing network Use underused summer dorms so students can access affordable housing at partner campuses near their internship site.
03
Require employer commute scores Employers should publish realistic commute data — time, cost, safety, transit options — before interns sign a lease in an unfamiliar city.
04
Build a verified internship housing platform A centralized, scam-free platform with verified listings, lease guidance, and student reviews so no one starts from scratch.
05
Expand remote and hybrid options Remote internships reduce geographic barriers. Employers should treat them as full opportunities, not second-tier fallbacks.
