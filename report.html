Introduction

The transition from college to career has become increasingly dependent on internships. What was once an optional opportunity to gain experience has evolved into an unofficial requirement for entry into the professional world. Yet access to internships is far from equal. For students from low-income backgrounds, the true cost of participation — including housing, transportation, food, and lost wages — creates barriers that wealthier peers often do not have to consider.

This report advocates that the internship housing crisis is more than just one isolated issue. It's a network of financial, geographic, racial, and institutional factors which determine who can afford to participate in professional opportunities.

In order to clearly outline these problems and potential solutions, this report is divided into two sections: problem statements that illuminate why interns can't equitably access opportunity, and solution proposals that universities, employers, and policymakers can actually enact. The goal is not simply to describe why internships are difficult for students, but to show how the current system rewards wealth over talent and why structural reforms are necessary.

Barriers to Internship Access

Financial barriers: the burden of unpaid and underpaid internships

Internships rarely pay well enough to cover the true cost of participation. Students often have to pay for rent, transportation, food, professional clothing, and other costs associated with living on their own, sometimes without earning a paycheck at all. Rather than putting everyone on a level playing field, financial support from family allows some students to take advantage of career-building opportunities while forcing low-income students to decline them.

Fair access to internships is critical because internships have become a cornerstone of the college-to-career pipeline. College students use internships to build resumes, references, networks, and prove themselves in the hope of full-time job offers. But when internships fail to provide a livable wage, they turn away students who cannot afford to work without compensation. The National Association of Colleges and Employers explains that "unpaid internships exacerbate equity concerns" because these opportunities are significantly more difficult to obtain for students who cannot afford to give up paid employment.

The expense of unpaid internships does not end with lost wages. Students must often pay for summer housing, transportation, food, professional clothes, and other basic living costs. According to the Center for Research on College-Workforce Transitions, unpaid internships frequently shift the burden of living expenses onto students and their families, making them inaccessible for low-income students. This problem becomes even more serious in high-cost cities, where many competitive internships are located.

Unpaid internships have also been linked with weaker long-term career outcomes. NACE found that paid interns from the Class of 2023 earned an average of 1.4 job offers upon graduating from college, while unpaid interns earned only 0.9 offers. If unpaid internships do not provide as strong a career benefit as paid internships but still require the same sacrifice of time, labor, and money, then internships can actually increase inequality instead of reducing it.

Geographic barriers: housing and commute problems in high-cost cities

The internship crisis is also a housing crisis. Many internships are concentrated in major urban areas where rent is high and short-term housing is difficult to find. The Bay Area, Los Angeles, New York City, and Washington, D.C. are among the most competitive internship markets, but they are also some of the least affordable places to live. Students who receive internships in these areas may be excited about the professional opportunity but quickly realize that housing costs make participation unrealistic.

On their own, interns frequently end up living in outer neighborhoods or lower-cost cities far from their workplace. They may sign leases without fully understanding whether the commute is realistic, safe, or affordable. A Congressional Research Service report on housing cost burdens also suggests that relocating farther away from job centers can create additional transportation expenses that offset the lower cost of housing. In other words, a cheaper apartment does not always mean a cheaper summer.

Employer support is often limited. A Cartus pulse survey reported by Relocity found that 57 percent of respondents were not assisting interns with housing. This means many interns must navigate unfamiliar rental markets alone — without knowing which neighborhoods are safe, which listings are legitimate, or how far a commute really is.

Racial and structural barriers in the internship pipeline

The internship system is not racially neutral. Low-income students, who are disproportionately Black, Hispanic, and first-generation, are often the students most likely to be excluded by unpaid or underpaid internships. When internships require students to relocate, pay rent, cover food, and give up summer wages, the opportunity becomes easier for students from wealthier families and harder for students from historically marginalized backgrounds.

The National Career Development Association emphasizes that students living in expensive urban areas and students from low-income families face the steepest financial barriers to participating in unpaid internships. This is not a minor inconvenience for a small group of students. It is a structural form of exclusion built into the geography of American opportunity. Labor economist Alan Seals captures this problem clearly, explaining that internships are often concentrated on the coasts — among the most expensive places in the country to live.

Georgetown University's Center on Education and the Workforce argues that competitive internships increasingly lock out low-income students because they are unpaid or offer insufficient compensation. Because those students cannot afford to participate, they graduate without internship experience and may then be disqualified from entry-level jobs that treat internship experience as a baseline expectation.

The 2025 Student Financial Wellness Survey, analyzing 24,361 caregiving undergraduates, found that Black students and Hispanic students reported higher rates of housing discrimination while searching for accommodations than White students. Students of color may face difficulty not only in affording housing near internship sites, but also in being treated fairly when they try to access that housing.

Institutional barriers: the fragmented housing search

Another major barrier is the fragmented process of finding internship housing. For students doing internships in urban areas, housing is often one of the most difficult problems to solve. Since there is no centralized platform or consistent support from internship companies or universities, students may have to spend hours comparing Facebook groups, Airbnb listings, Reddit posts, university housing boards, and informal sublet pages.

Short-term housing creates another problem. Many internships last only a few weeks or a few months, but many landlords prefer longer leases. Students may be forced to split apartments with strangers, accept expensive short-term rentals, live far from the internship site, or stay in hotels. Each option creates new costs and risks.

In addition, students often do not receive enough support from universities or internship companies. Even when internships are paid, students still have to manage housing, transportation, groceries, safety, health needs, and the stress of adjusting to a new working environment. Housing is viewed as an individual student problem — but it's a systemic one.

Proposals for a More Equitable Internship System

01

Require internships to be paid or fully funded

The most direct solution is to create a paid-or-funded internship standard. Universities should not allow students to take internships for credit unless the internship either pays at least minimum wage or provides a guaranteed stipend that covers basic costs such as housing, food, and transportation. There is already a legal framework for this: the U.S. Department of Labor uses the "primary beneficiary test" under the Fair Labor Standards Act to determine whether an intern at a for-profit employer is actually an employee who must be paid. For nonprofit, public service, or small-company internships that genuinely cannot afford full wages, universities should create need-based internship grants reserved for low-income and first-generation students.

02

Create a university-to-university summer housing network

Many universities have dorms or campus apartments that are underused during the summer. At the same time, students from other campuses often need temporary housing for internships nearby. Universities should establish a centralized summer housing exchange where students going on internships can receive dormitory-style housing at partner institutions near their internship location. The University of California system could be a strong model — a UCSD student interning in Los Angeles could use UCLA summer housing, a UC Berkeley student interning in San Diego could use UCSD housing. This model already has a partial example in the UCDC program, which provides structured housing for UC students doing internships in Washington, D.C.

03

Require employer commute scores and housing guidance

Employers should provide interns with a commute score before students sign a lease. This commute score would show how realistic it is to get from a housing location to the internship site using public transportation, walking, biking, driving, or shuttle service — including estimated commute time, cost, safety considerations, and whether late-night or early-morning transportation is available. Employers should also provide a short list of vetted housing options near the workplace. This proposal is realistic because it does not require every employer to build housing — it simply requires employers to provide practical information and transportation support.

04

Build a centralized and verified internship housing platform

Students need a reliable and centralized housing platform designed specifically for interns. Instead of searching across Facebook groups, Reddit, Airbnb, and informal sublet pages, students should have access to a verified platform that lists safe, short-term, affordable housing options near internship sites. The platform should include verified listings, estimated commute times, lease length, price, safety information, and roommate preferences. Universities could also use this platform to build a database of previous student housing experiences — so that former interns' knowledge is retained and passed on, rather than every student learning everything from scratch.

05

Expand remote and hybrid internship options

Employers should expand remote and hybrid internship options when work can be completed effectively outside the office. Remote internships are not perfect for every field, but they can reduce geographic barriers for students who cannot afford to relocate. Remote internships shouldn't be viewed as second-tier opportunities — employers can ensure they are still valuable through mentorship, clearly directed projects, regular check-ins, skill-building workshops, and networking opportunities. Hybrid options can also help: an internship could require students to be on site for one week of training or a few key events, while allowing the rest of the work to be remote.

Conclusion

Internships should be a gateway to professional opportunity, not a privilege reserved for students who can afford to work for free or relocate to expensive cities. As this report demonstrates, the barriers to equitable internship access are financial, geographic, racial, and institutional, and they compound on one another at every stage. Low-income students are priced out before they apply, pushed into unsafe or inconvenient housing, and left without adequate support from universities or employers.

The solutions exist. Internships should be paid or fully funded. Universities should create summer housing networks that make use of existing dorm space. Employers should provide commute scores, vetted housing guidance, transit support, and realistic information before interns sign leases. Universities and employers should also build centralized housing platforms and expand remote or hybrid internship options where possible.

The internship housing crisis isn't just a hardship for students. It's an access issue rippling through the college-to-career pipeline. As long as internships are not universally available to students regardless of financial situation, the professional pipeline will continue to sift students based on wealth rather than merit. An equitable internship ecosystem will consider housing, transportation, and pay as foundational elements of access rather than afterthoughts students should navigate on their own.
